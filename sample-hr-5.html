The Resignation Nobody Saw Coming Is Never Actually a Surprise

Brief: Monthly insight newsletter written for PeopleFirst Consulting, a fictional HR consultancy. Written for HR managers, founders, and operations leads who have experienced unexpected resignations and want to understand the early warning signs they are consistently missing.

Monthly Insight Newsletter Written for HR Managers and Founders

Subject: The resignation nobody saw coming is never actually a surprise

Preview text: The signals were there. They usually are.

Hi there,

Last month a founder I know lost her head of product.

No warning. No counter-offer conversation. Just a resignation letter on a Monday morning and a two-week notice that felt like it came from nowhere.

She called me that afternoon. The first thing she said was that she had no idea he was unhappy.

The second thing she said, after we had talked for about twenty minutes, was that she had actually known for months. She just had not known what she was seeing.

This happens more than most leaders want to admit.

The signals that are easy to miss

Disengagement rarely arrives as a resignation letter. It arrives as something quieter first.

A team member who used to push back in meetings stops having opinions. Someone who was always the first to volunteer for new projects starts waiting to be asked. A person who used to send messages at 7 AM because they were excited about something stops sending them at all.

None of these things look like someone who is about to leave. They look like someone having a quiet week. Or a difficult month. Or adjusting to a new workload.

They are also, according to a 2024 study published in the Journal of Applied Psychology, among the most statistically reliable early indicators that an employee is actively considering leaving.

The study followed 248 employees across 14 organisations over 18 months. The three strongest predictors of voluntary turnover were reduced discretionary effort, decreased proactive communication, and withdrawal from non-mandatory team activities.

None of those things required an exit interview to detect. They were all visible in the weeks and months before the resignation.

Why managers miss them

Two reasons, both understandable and both fixable.

The first is that most managers are not taught to look for these signals. They are taught to manage performance, hit targets, and handle conflict. Watching for the early signs of disengagement is not part of most management training because most management training was designed for factories not knowledge workers.

The second is that when you are busy, quieter behaviour from a team member feels like a relief rather than a warning sign. One fewer person asking questions. One fewer voice in the meeting. That relief has a cost that shows up later.

What actually helps

The companies with the lowest voluntary turnover are not the ones with the most competitive salaries. Research from MIT Sloan Management Review found that toxic culture is 10.4 times more likely to predict voluntary turnover than compensation.

What they have instead is a consistent practice of structured one-on-ones where managers are trained to ask questions that surface how someone is actually doing, not just what they are working on.

Not "how is the project going" but "what part of your work is feeling most draining right now" and "is there anything you need that you are not getting."

These conversations feel awkward at first. They get easier. And they surface the information that makes the Monday morning resignation letter far less likely.

One thing to try this week

If you manage people directly, pick one person on your team who has been quieter than usual over the last few weeks. Not someone in conflict or underperforming. Just quieter.

Schedule a 20-minute conversation with no agenda except to ask how they are doing and to listen to the answer without immediately problem-solving.

That conversation might reveal nothing. Or it might be the one that changes what happens three months from now.

Until next month.

The PeopleFirst Team
