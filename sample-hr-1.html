What Companies Get Wrong About Talent Retention

Brief: An industry insight post written for an HR professional examining why most companies confuse hiring problems with retention problems, and what the best organizations do differently in the first 90 days.

Most companies do not have a hiring problem.

They have a retention problem they keep trying to solve by hiring more people.

I have watched organizations spend months recruiting someone, celebrate their first day, then lose them fourteen months later with no real understanding of what went wrong. The exit interview happens. HR takes notes. Leadership nods. And three weeks later the same role is posted on the same job board with the same description.

Nothing changes because nobody looked at the right problem.

The truth is most employees do not leave because of money. They leave because somewhere between the offer letter and month six, the experience stopped matching the promise. The manager was too stretched to invest in them. The role turned out to be different from what was sold in the interview. The growth path that was mentioned casually during hiring never materialized into anything real.

And the employee, who came in genuinely motivated, started making peace with leaving long before they handed in their resignation.

Retention does not start at the exit interview. It starts at the offer letter.

The companies holding onto their best people right now are not doing anything complicated. They are checking in consistently. They are delivering on what they promised during hiring. They are making sure managers have the time and the skill to actually lead their teams, not just manage their output.

If your onboarding ends at week two, your offboarding starts at week three.

Fix what happens inside the building. The hiring problem outside will take care of itself.

What is one thing your company does in the first ninety days that actually makes new hires want to stay?
