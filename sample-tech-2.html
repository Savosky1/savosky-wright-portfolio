The Software Feature Nobody Asked For

Brief: A LinkedIn post written for a tech founder examining why the most successful products are built around removing friction rather than adding features and what that means for how teams should be building in 2026.

The most successful tech products of the last decade did not win because they had the most features.

They won because they had the fewest.

Notion started as a simple note tool. Slack started as a messaging box. Stripe started as two lines of code that let you accept payments. None of them launched with everything. They launched with one thing that worked so well people could not stop talking about it.

The teams building those products understood something most product managers still argue against in sprint planning meetings.

Every feature you add is a decision your user has to make.

Every additional button, every extra setting, every optional configuration is friction. And friction is the thing that turns a product people were excited about into a product people stop opening.

The question that should be asked before every feature gets prioritized is not can we build this. It is what does the user have to think about if we do.

The products winning in 2026 are not the ones with the longest feature lists. They are the ones where every interaction feels like it was designed by someone who genuinely understood what the user was trying to accomplish and removed everything standing between them and that outcome.

Less is not a design philosophy. It is a growth strategy.

What is one feature in a product you use daily that you wish simply did not exist?
